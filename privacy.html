1) Quick Summary

Legal advice: The Constitution recognises privacy as a fundamental right under Article 43. However, this is limited in scope to the privacy of correspondence and other means of communication, and the protection of one’s ‘home’ against entry, search and seizure. This right is subject to reasonable restrictions imposed by law in the interests of the security of the State, public order, morality, or health. Federal laws Cyber Security Act, 2023 (CSA) The CSA replaces the Digital Security Act of 2018 (DSA) but retains most of its provisions and makes only minor amendments. It prohibits the collection, selling, possession, provision, or usage of ‘identity information’ of a person, institution, company or any entity created by law (collectively termed as “person”), by another person without lawful authority. Identity information is defined as “any external, biological or physical information or any other information which singly or jointly can identify a person or a system”. These include name, photograph, address, physical or digital signature, national identity card, fingerprint, voice print, retina image, iris image, DNA profile, or any other identification which might be available with the advancement of technology. The CSA penalises offences such as illegally accessing critical information infrastructure and computers, illegally holding or transferring data owned by the government and financial authorities, among others. As per the CSA, the Government may, if it deems it necessary, preserve any information saved in a computer in interest of an investigation under the Act. The CSA, like its predecessor allows law enforcement officials to conduct searches, seizures, and arrests without warrants when the relevant officer has reasonable grounds to believe that an offence under the Act has been committed or is likely to be committed, or that evidence may be lost, altered, or rendered unavailable. The Information and Communication Technology Act, 2006 (ICT Act) The ICT Act was enacted to regulate information and communication technology in Bangladesh. It lays down offences such as breach of confidentiality and privacy by the disclosure of private information, among others. Section 46 of the ICT Act provides interception rights to the government in the interests of security of state and public order, among others. The Penal Code, 1860 The Penal Code, 1860 lays down offences punishable by imprisonment and fine. Section 509 of the Penal Code explicitly punishes the violation of the privacy of women with imprisonment and/or fine. The Bangladesh Telecommunication Act, 2001 (BTA) The BTA was enacted to regulate telecommunications systems and services in Bangladesh. The Bangladesh Telecommunication Regulatory Commission (BTRC) established under the Act is vested with, among other functions, ensuring the protection of the privacy of telecommunications under Section 30 of the BTA. Further, Section 67(B) protects telecommunications from interception or divulgence by any third-party. However, Section 97(A) (introduced in the Bangladesh Telecommunications (Amendment) Act 2006) grants exceptions for interception by the State, on the grounds of national security and public order. The State may also require the telecommunications operator to retain records of specific users on the same grounds.
